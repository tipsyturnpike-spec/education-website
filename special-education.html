ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)

Understanding ADHD

ADHD is not a lack of attention - it's difficulty regulating attention. Students with ADHD may struggle with focus, impulsivity, hyperactivity, or a combination. They often think divergently, notice things others miss, and have tremendous energy when engaged.

Early Childhood (Ages 0-5)

Understanding the Child

At this age, ADHD may appear as constant movement, difficulty sitting still for stories, quick transitions between activities, and intense reactions. This is not defiance - it's neurological.

Key Accommodations

Movement breaks every 10-15 min Sensory seating options Visual schedules Clear, simple instructions Immediate feedback

üå± Hands-On Activity: Moving Garden

Standard Activity: Planting seeds in one spot.

ADHD-Adapted: Create a "garden circuit" - child moves between stations: dig here, water there, drop seeds at next spot, cover at another. Movement is built into the learning.

Why it works: Allows movement while accomplishing the same task. Channels energy productively.

üî® Hands-On Activity: Tool Exploration

Standard Activity: Sit and watch tool demonstration.

ADHD-Adapted: Let child hold each tool while you explain. Let them tap it, feel it, move with it. Short bursts of instruction followed by immediate hands-on practice.

üìö Hands-On Activity: Story Time

Standard Activity: Sit and listen to story.

ADHD-Adapted: Act out the story while reading. Child becomes the characters, moves through the plot physically. Story sticks when body is involved.

Elementary (Ages 6-10)

Understanding the Child

School demands increase. Child may be labeled "behavior problem" for fidgeting, interrupting, or forgetting. They often feel bad about themselves. They need success experiences, not punishment.

Key Accommodations

Preferred seating near teacher Fidget tools allowed Break tasks into chunks Timers for focus periods Movement as reward

üßÆ Math: Multiplication That Moves

Standard: Worksheets.

ADHD-Adapted: Create a multiplication hopscotch. Draw grid on playground. Child jumps to answers. Or use rhythm and clapping patterns for times tables. Body learns what brain struggles with.

üî¨ Science: Experiment Stations

Standard: One experiment, all watch.

ADHD-Adapted: Set up 5 experiment stations. Students rotate every 7 minutes. ADHD child gets novelty, movement, and still completes all experiments.

üìù Writing: Active Composition

Standard: Sit and write.

ADHD-Adapted: Act out story first. Use whiteboards standing up. Try writing on windows with dry-erase markers. Change position frequently. Typing may work better than handwriting.

Middle School (Ages 11-13)

Understanding the Child

Executive function demands increase. Organization, planning, time management become critical. ADHD students often fall behind not because they can't understand, but because they can't organize.

Key Accommodations

Written AND verbal instructions Check-in halfway through tasks Extra time for organization Movement-friendly furniture No penalty for fidgeting

üõ†Ô∏è Woodshop: Building with Purpose

Standard: Follow written plans.

ADHD-Adapted: ADHD students often excel in woodshop - it's hands-on, visible progress, concrete results. Break project into small steps with clear stopping points. Use checklists. Celebrate completion.

üìä Math: Real Projects

Standard: Abstract algebra problems.

ADHD-Adapted: ADHD brains crave relevance. Teach algebra through building a skateboard ramp, budgeting for a project, calculating sports statistics. When math matters, focus improves.

High School (Ages 14-18)

Understanding the Young Adult

Self-awareness grows. Many ADHD students have developed coping strategies. They need support in executive function while honoring their different brain wiring.

Key Accommodations

Assistive technology Extended time on tasks Option for movement breaks Choice in demonstration of learning

üîß Auto Mechanics: Perfect for ADHD

Auto mechanics is often IDEAL for ADHD students. It's hands-on, problem-solving, varied tasks, immediate feedback. Many ADHD adults become excellent mechanics, electricians, builders - careers that value their wiring.

üìù Self-Advocacy Skills

Teach students to understand their own ADHD. What helps them focus? What environments work? How do they learn best? This self-knowledge is more valuable than any accommodation.

Autism Spectrum Disorder

Understanding Autism

Autism is a neurodevelopmental difference affecting communication, social interaction, and sensory processing. Every autistic person is unique. Strengths often include pattern recognition, deep focus on interests, honesty, and attention to detail.

Early Childhood (Ages 0-5)

Understanding the Child

May show differences in eye contact, response to name, play patterns. May have intense interests, sensory sensitivities (to sounds, textures, lights). May communicate differently - some nonverbal, some with advanced vocabulary.

Key Accommodations

Predictable routines Visual schedules Sensory-friendly environment Alternative communication (PECS, sign) Prepare for transitions

üå± Gardening: Sensory Garden

Standard: Plant seeds in dirt.

Autism-Adapted: Create a sensory garden with plants of different textures (lamb's ear - soft), smells (mint, lavender), colors. Let child explore at their own pace. Some may love the feel of soil; others may need gloves. Honor sensory needs.

üî® Tool Play: Structured Exploration

Many autistic children love order and systems. Present tools in organized trays. Demonstrate exactly where each belongs. Some may line them up - that's okay. Predictability is calming.

üìö Communication Through Interest

If child is obsessed with trains, use trains to teach everything. Count trains, read about trains, build trains. Connection happens through their interests.

Elementary (Ages 6-10)

Understanding the Child

Social demands increase. Child may struggle with group work, recess, understanding social cues. May have intense interests. May experience sensory overload in busy classrooms.

Key Accommodations

Quiet space for breaks Social stories Explicit social instruction No forced eye contact Interest-based learning

üßÆ Math: Pattern Recognition

Many autistic children excel at patterns. Use this strength. Teach math through patterns, sequences, systems. Let them create their own patterns. This is their superpower.

üåç Social Studies: System Understanding

Autistic minds often understand systems deeply. Teach how governments work as systems, how economies function. This approach makes abstract concepts concrete.

ü§ù Social Skills Through Shared Interests

Create interest-based groups - train club, drawing club, building club. Social skills develop naturally when children share passion, not through forced interaction.

Middle School (Ages 11-13)

Understanding the Child

Social awareness grows. Autistic students may feel more different. Bullying risk increases. Self-advocacy becomes important. Many discover their autistic identity.

Key Accommodations

Safe adult to check in with Lunch bunch options Clear expectations Sensory breaks as needed

üõ†Ô∏è Woodshop: Detail Work

Many autistic students excel at detailed, precise work. Woodshop can be a place of pride. They may measure more accurately, notice details others miss. Celebrate these strengths.

High School (Ages 14-18)

Understanding the Young Adult

Many embrace their autistic identity. Self-advocacy skills are crucial. Planning for post-secondary - some will go to college, some to trade school, some directly to work.

Key Accommodations

Self-advocacy training Career exploration based on strengths Social groups with similar interests

üîß Auto Mechanics: System Thinkers

Autistic individuals often excel at understanding complex systems. Auto mechanics, computer programming, engineering are natural fits. Provide opportunities to dive deep into these areas.

üìù College/Career Prep

Help students understand their learning needs. Practice requesting accommodations. Explore careers that value autistic strengths - attention to detail, honesty, deep focus, pattern recognition.

Physical Disabilities

Understanding Physical Disabilities

Physical disabilities include cerebral palsy, spina bifida, muscular dystrophy, spinal cord injuries, limb differences, and mobility impairments. These affect movement, but not intelligence. Our curriculum is designed to be fully accessible to all bodies.

Early Childhood (Ages 0-5)

Understanding the Child

Children with physical disabilities explore the world differently. They may need adaptive equipment to sit, move, or manipulate objects. Their cognitive development follows same patterns as peers - they just need different ways to interact.

Key Accommodations

Adaptive seating Accessible play spaces Switch-adapted toys Positioning equipment Extra time for exploration

üå± Gardening: Accessible Gardens

Standard: Ground-level garden beds.

Adapted: Create raised garden beds at wheelchair height. Use vertical gardens for children who can't bend. Provide adapted tools with easy-grip handles. Every child can plant, water, and harvest when the garden comes to them.

üî® Tool Exploration: Adapted Tools

Provide tools with built-up handles, straps, or switches. A child with limited hand use can still hammer with a adapted mallet. A child who can't hold a paintbrush can use a head pointer or mouth stick. Creativity removes barriers.

üìö Story Time: Positioning for Participation

Ensure child is comfortably positioned - in stander, on wedge, in adapted chair - so they can fully participate. Use eye gaze boards for children who can't point. Every child can choose which book to read.

Elementary (Ages 6-10)

Understanding the Child

School environment may present physical barriers. Fatigue can be an issue. Child may need assistance with mobility, writing, or manipulation. Focus on independence and self-advocacy.

Key Accommodations

Accessible classroom layout Assistive technology Speech-to-text software Alternative writing tools Extra time for tasks

üßÆ Math: Manipulatives for All

Provide magnetic numbers for students who can't hold small pieces. Use large, lightweight manipulatives. Try digital math tools with switch access. Math understanding doesn't require fine motor skills.

üî¨ Science: Adapted Experiments

Set up experiments so child can observe and participate. Use tube testers, adapted lab equipment. Partner with peer for two-person tasks where child directs, peer executes. Leadership builds confidence.

üõ†Ô∏è Woodshop: Universal Design

Workbenches at wheelchair height. Adapted clamps to hold materials. Power tools with easy-grip switches. Students can design and direct even if they need assistance with some physical tasks.

Middle School (Ages 11-13)

Understanding the Child

Self-advocacy becomes crucial. Student should know their equipment, understand their needs, and be able to communicate them. Social inclusion is vital - physical differences can lead to isolation.

Key Accommodations

Self-advocacy training Peer mentoring Assistive tech training Accessible field trips

üîß Mechanics: Accessible Auto Shop

Work with physical therapists to find positions that work - sitting, standing with support, using adapted tools. Many people with physical disabilities work successfully in trades with proper accommodations.

üìù Writing: Alternative Methods

Teach touch-typing if handwriting is difficult. Voice-to-text software improves. Some students use eye gaze systems. Focus on ideas and content, not method of production.

High School (Ages 14-18)

Understanding the Young Adult

Transition planning is critical. Explore careers, college accommodations, independent living skills. Student should understand ADA rights and how to request accommodations.

Key Accommodations

Transition planning Career exploration College visit planning Independent living skills

üí∞ Financial: Managing Personal Care

Teach budgeting for personal care attendants, equipment maintenance, medical expenses. Real-world financial skills specific to life with a disability.

üîß Career Exploration

Connect with adults with similar disabilities who work in fields of interest. Virtual job shadows, interviews, mentorships. Show what's possible.

Intellectual Disabilities

Understanding Intellectual Disabilities

Intellectual disabilities affect cognitive functioning and adaptive behavior. This includes Down syndrome, Fragile X, and many other conditions. Learning may take longer, but every person can learn, grow, and contribute meaningfully.

Early Childhood (Ages 0-5)

Understanding the Child

Development may follow same sequence but at slower pace. Celebrate every milestone. Focus on what child CAN do. Early intervention is crucial.

Key Accommodations

Repetition and routine Multi-sensory teaching Break tasks into steps Immediate positive feedback

üå± Gardening: One Step at a Time

Teach gardening in tiny steps. Today: touch the soil. Next week: put seed in soil. Next: cover it. Celebrate each step. The garden doesn't need to be perfect - the process matters.

üî® Tool Play: Mastery Through Repetition

Practice same tool skill many times. Child may need 100 repetitions to master what another learns in 10. That's okay. Mastery builds confidence and real skill.

Elementary (Ages 6-10)

Understanding the Child

Academic learning may be slower. Focus on functional skills alongside academics. Reading, writing, math that apply to daily life. Social inclusion is crucial.

Key Accommodations

Modified materials Visual supports Peer buddies Life skills integration

üßÆ Math: Real-Life Math

Count snack items. Measure ingredients. Set table for correct number of people. Math that matters for daily living is highly motivating.

üç≥ Cooking: Independence Through Recipes

Use picture recipes. Step-by-step cards. Practice same recipe many times until independent. Cooking is a lifelong skill worth the time.

Middle School (Ages 11-13)

Understanding the Child

Focus shifts to functional independence and community participation. Vocational awareness begins. Social connections remain vital.

Key Accommodations

Community-based instruction Job sampling Social skills groups Daily living skills

üí∞ Money: Community Shopping

Practice money skills in real stores. Make small purchases. Count change. Real-world practice beats worksheets every time.

üîß Pre-Vocational Skills

Try different hands-on tasks - gardening, woodshop, kitchen, cleaning. Find what student enjoys and can develop into future skills.

High School (Ages 14-18)

Understanding the Young Adult

Transition to adulthood is primary focus. Job training, independent living, community participation, relationships. Quality of life is the goal.

Key Accommodations

Job coaching Community-based instruction Self-determination training Adult service connection

üîß Work-Based Learning

Paid or volunteer work experiences. Start with school-based jobs (cafeteria, office, grounds). Move to community placements with support. Real work, real pride.

üè° Independent Living

Practice cooking, cleaning, laundry, budgeting in real settings. Apartment or house on school grounds provides safe practice. Independence is the goal.

Specific Learning Disabilities

Understanding Learning Disabilities

Learning disabilities affect how the brain processes information. This includes dyslexia (reading), dyscalculia (math), dysgraphia (writing). Intelligence is average or above - the brain is simply wired differently.

Dyslexia (Reading)

Understanding

Difficulty with phonological processing, word recognition, spelling. NOT about intelligence. Many brilliant minds have dyslexia.

Accommodations

Audiobooks, text-to-speech, extra time, multisensory instruction, decodable texts, no public reading unless voluntary.

üìö Literature: Accessible Reading

Provide audiobooks for same literature peers read. Student can listen while following along. Comprehension is the goal - decoding is just one path.

Dyscalculia (Math)

Understanding

Difficulty with number sense, math facts, calculation, spatial reasoning. Not about effort.

Accommodations

Calculator use, manipulatives, graph paper for organization, extra time, real-world math focus.

üßÆ Math: Real Numbers

Teach math through woodworking (measurement), cooking (fractions), money (decimals). Concrete before abstract.

Dysgraphia (Writing)

Understanding

Difficulty with handwriting, spelling, organizing ideas on paper. Physical act of writing may be painful.

Accommodations

Speech-to-text, typing, dictation, graphic organizers, reduced written output requirements.

üìù Writing: Voice First

Let student dictate ideas. They can see their words appear on screen. Content is what matters - handwriting is just one method.

Speech & Language Disorders

Understanding Communication Disorders

Includes articulation disorders, fluency disorders (stuttering), voice disorders, and language disorders (receptive/expressive). Difficulty communicating does NOT mean difficulty thinking.

Key Principles

  • ‚Ä¢ Never interrupt or finish sentences
  • ‚Ä¢ Give unhurried time to respond
  • ‚Ä¢ Accept all communication methods
  • ‚Ä¢ Model, don't correct

Accommodations

AAC devices Picture exchange Sign language Extra response time

üå± Gardening: Non-Verbal Participation

Child can participate fully without speaking. Point to what they want to do next. Use picture cards for choices. All communication is valid.

üîß Woodshop: Doing Speaks

Many children with speech disorders excel in hands-on work where communication isn't primary. Let their work speak for them.

Deaf & Hard of Hearing

Understanding Hearing Differences

The Deaf community has its own culture and language (ASL). Hearing levels vary from mild loss to profound deafness. Visual access is crucial.

Key Principles

  • ‚Ä¢ Face student when speaking
  • ‚Ä¢ Ensure lighting on face
  • ‚Ä¢ Use visual supports
  • ‚Ä¢ Learn basic signs

Accommodations

ASL interpreter FM system Captioned videos Visual alerts

üîß Auto Mechanics: Visual Learning

Many Deaf individuals excel in visual-spatial trades. Demonstrate, don't just explain. Written instructions, diagrams, hands-on practice work well.

üå± Gardening: See the Process

Gardening is naturally visual. Show seed, show planted seed, show sprout. The process is visible. Great learning for visual learners.

Blind & Visually Impaired

Understanding Visual Impairments

Range from low vision to total blindness. Learning happens through touch, hearing, and other senses. Braille literacy is crucial for many.

Key Principles

  • ‚Ä¢ Describe clearly (don't point)
  • ‚Ä¢ Use hands-on exploration
  • ‚Ä¢ Maintain consistent environment
  • ‚Ä¢ Teach orientation & mobility

Accommodations

Braille materials Audio description Large print Tactile graphics

üå± Gardening: Tactile Garden

Create garden with plants of different textures. Use raised beds at comfortable height. Feel the soil, feel the leaves, smell the herbs. Gardening is sensory, not just visual.

üõ†Ô∏è Woodshop: Feel the Work

Many blind individuals become excellent woodworkers. Teach through hands-on guidance. Use tactile measuring tools. Feel the wood grain, feel the cut. Safety first - clear organization of tools.

üîß Auto Mechanics: Feel and Hear

Diagnose by sound, by feel, by smell. Many mechanics close their eyes to focus on these senses anyway. Blind mechanics can excel with proper training.

Emotional & Behavioral Disorders

Understanding Emotional & Behavioral Needs

Includes anxiety disorders, depression, bipolar disorder, conduct disorders, PTSD, and more. Behavior is communication. These students need support, not punishment.

Key Principles

  • ‚Ä¢ Build trusting relationships first
  • ‚Ä¢ Predictable routines reduce anxiety
  • ‚Ä¢ Teach self-regulation skills
  • ‚Ä¢ Behavior is communication

Accommodations

Quiet space for breaks Sensory tools Check-in system Flexible deadlines

üå± Gardening: Therapeutic by Nature

Gardening is inherently calming. Soil contact increases serotonin. Watching things grow builds hope. Responsibility builds self-worth. Perfect for emotional regulation.

üõ†Ô∏è Woodshop: Channeling Energy

Physical work releases energy and stress. Creating something tangible builds self-esteem. Following steps provides structure. Can be highly therapeutic.

üîß Auto Mechanics: Problem-Solving Focus

Diagnosing and fixing provides sense of control and mastery. Great for students who feel helpless in other areas.

üç≥ Cooking: Nurturing Self and Others

Cooking for others builds connection. Learning to nourish self builds independence. Immediate sensory rewards (smell, taste) provide comfort.

Multiple Disabilities & Complex Needs

Understanding Complex Needs

Some students have combinations of disabilities - physical, intellectual, sensory, medical. Each student is unique. The goal is always quality of life, communication, and meaningful participation.

Key Principles

  • ‚Ä¢ Presume competence
  • ‚Ä¢ Find communication method
  • ‚Ä¢ Celebrate small responses
  • ‚Ä¢ Medical needs come first

Approaches

Sensory stimulation Switch access Positioning for participation Eye gaze technology

üå± Sensory Garden

Plants with different textures, smells, colors. Student can experience even if they can't actively plant. Position them to touch, smell, see (if possible). Every child deserves nature.

üî® Adapted Tool Use

With proper positioning and adapted switches, many students can activate tools. Even pressing a switch to start a drill provides sense of agency and participation.

üìö Story Time

Read aloud with sensory experiences. When character touches something soft, student touches soft fabric. When it rains, mist water. Learning through all senses.

Traumatic Brain Injury

Understanding TBI

TBI affects each person differently depending on injury location. Student may have been typical before injury and now has new challenges. Memory, attention, processing speed, and behavior may be affected.

Key Principles

  • ‚Ä¢ Be patient with memory
  • ‚Ä¢ Reduce cognitive load
  • ‚Ä¢ Watch for fatigue
  • ‚Ä¢ Build on strengths

Accommodations

Memory supports Frequent breaks Reduced distractions Consistent routines

Universal Design for Learning

Design for Everyone from the Start

When we design for accessibility, everyone benefits. These principles should be built into every activity, not added as an afterthought.

Multiple Means of Engagement

Offer choices, connect to interests, provide authentic tasks. When students care, they learn.

Multiple Means of Representation

Present information in multiple ways - visual, auditory, tactile, kinesthetic. No single method works for all.

Multiple Means of Expression

Let students show learning in different ways - speaking, writing, building, drawing, performing. Assess the learning, not the method.

üå± Garden Project - Universal Design

Engagement: Students choose what to plant based on interests (food, flowers, herbs).
Representation: Show planting through demonstration, pictures, written steps, and verbal instruction.
Expression: Students show learning by harvesting, drawing, writing, photographing, or teaching others.
